\section{Control structure and strategy [cite: 3]}

\subsection{Preliminary Analysis [cite: 4]}
Initially, the design was based on the standard criteria suggested in the handout. [cite: 5] However, the resulting response was not entirely satisfactory: the system exhibited a highly restrictive trade-off between speed and oscillations, failing to fully exploit the physical limits of the motor to minimize the rise time. [cite: 6] To overcome these limitations, we directly modified the pole placement of the extended system, aiming for a more aggressive yet controlled configuration. [cite: 7]

\subsection{Controller Structure and Anti-Windup Mechanism [cite: 8]}
To address the challenge, a state-space control strategy featuring integral action combined with an anti-windup mechanism was adopted. [cite: 9] The inclusion of the anti-windup scheme is essential because, in the attempt to drastically reduce the rise time, the controller frequently demands voltages that exceed the physical limits of the actuator (saturation). [cite: 10] Without this solution, the integrator would continue to accumulate the tracking error even when the motor is already operating at maximum power (the windup phenomenon). [cite: 11] This would result in massive overshoot and prolonged settling times once the system re-enters its linear operating region. [cite: 12] The local feedback loop of the anti-windup effectively "brakes" the integrator during saturation, ensuring it remains responsive as soon as the error decreases. [cite: 13] 

The control system relies on an extended state model $\Sigma_{e}=(A_{e},B_{e},C_{e},0)$ that incorporates the integrated error variable $x_{I}=\int(y-r)d\tau$ to guarantee asymptotic tracking of step references and the rejection of constant disturbances. [cite: 14] The anti-windup mechanism enables the use of high gains, meaning the closed-loop system poles can be placed significantly far from the imaginary axis (i.e., with a highly negative real part). [cite: 15]

\subsection{Pole Placement Logic [cite: 16]}
The pole placement logic was tuned to achieve a dominant first-order behavior. [cite: 17] Rather than opting for a pair of complex conjugate poles with low damping (typical of a second-order approximation) which, despite being fast, would introduce oscillations and risk violating the $M_{p}\le10\%$ constraint-we chose to make a real pole predominant. [cite: 18] This design choice yields an exponential-type response that is inherently free of oscillations. [cite: 19]

\section{Controller Parameters [cite: 20]}
The pole placement was performed using the MATLAB acker function. [cite: 21] The chosen values for the closed-loop system eigenvalues $(A_{e}-B_{e}K_{e})$ are as follows: [cite: 22]
\begin{itemize}
    \item Complex conjugate poles: $\lambda_{1,2}=-80\pm17j$ [cite: 23]
    \item Integrator real pole: $\lambda_{3}=-11$ [cite: 24]
\end{itemize}
